sisters are for others

If there weren’t so many temptations in the world, things might have ended up differently. My sister would be inclined to agree, or at least I’d like to think so. After all, we were quite close before she escaped the unbearable Arizona heat for Swarthmore College.

“Good luck,” I said to her. Watched her skinny arms lift the luggage out of the car. “I’m always in your corner.”

She just smiled.

I meant to check in with her at some point, but things got busy and we lost contact over the course of the autumn semester. Me, consumed by college applications and pendulum-like grades, and her, settling into that new life of hers. I didn’t think too much of it until our father had brought up the question of her coming back for winter break, both him and I expecting her to tell us a date, eager to have someone other than each other in our hollowed, empty house but instead were given a single word: “Can’t.”

She was always funny, but never funny like that. There was nothing that couldn’t be done or undone. She should have known that more than anyone. That single word irked me and my throat felt raw for the following few days.

I was curious as to what made college so grand for her, enough to abandon me back home. Swarthmore became a mild obsession of mine, but one that I never mentioned to her. Somewhat out of a fear of retaliation and somewhat out of a sick pleasure, knowing something that she didn’t. Something she couldn’t do anything about.

The mystic air surrounding her college drew out my unbridled curiosity and I couldn’t, nor did I want to, stay away. I submitted my application one Tuesday morning and prayed.

The news of my acceptance came first. I was delighted and spent the day pacing my room with the local alternative rock station blasting from my computer speakers and thinking of ways I could tell my sister that I would be on campus with her next fall. The two of us, together again. I recited my words over and over again, trying different tones and cadences, wanting to find the perfect medium between uninterested and happenstance.

A call from the administration came the morning after. My sister was found in her dorm room, with one of our father’s ties wrapped around her neck.

My father couldn’t bear to fly us out to Pennsylvania, instead opting to ship the body and all of her belongings directly home. She didn’t have much stuff. Everything from her college life fit neatly in three medium-sized cardboard boxes. There wasn’t even a note. Something about that made me sick, rotten to my core. I suddenly felt detached from her, like I hadn’t known her my whole life. And maybe I didn’t.

 

There were no comments made when I decided to go to Swarthmore. By then, my father had become a shell of a human, more than he was before. I suppose it’s because he never really loved me and I never really loved him, both of us knowing and accepting this unconditionally. So I guess he truly didn’t have anybody left.

After her death, I tried to find clues of why she did what she had done. I tore apart those boxes and spiral notebooks trying to search for some semblance of an explanation. Some letter, or code to me. She was sly like that. But most of it was philosophical garbage from her classes. Nietzsche, Kant, all that bullshit.

My sister had always been a private person. It was because of this behavior that I sought out a way to connect to her removed from our gridlocked conversations. When I was thirteen, I had found her diary, disguised as an old math notebook buried within her underwear drawer. She was much more clever than she had ever led me to believe and the thought of that made my head float. I read her diary like it was the Bible, memorizing every detestable detail until it consumed me. A few days later, she left me a creased corner pointing to my name. This was the start of our play, our halcyon age.

She was never impulsive, which meant that every move up until her death was premeditated. In some sick way, I wondered if our last conversation on the phone had been part of her decision, if she even thought of me. After all, it was right before the incident.

I had called her spontaneously in the dead of night because I was feeling ill as the application season was coming to a crux, expecting her monotonous voicemail to blare back at me. She had a bad habit of not answering my calls, but would always leave some short voicemail to sate my worries. As the dial tone rang, I dozed off, trying to think of what I would say, what I wanted to say to get her to call me back, but then she picked up. There was a certain breathiness on the other line that made my hands go numb.

“Hello?”

My brain short-circuited. “Oh, hi. Is this a bad time?”

There was a cacophony of static. “I’m so glad you called me back.” I heard some muffled talking.

“What?”

She didn’t respond for a few beats. I called her name, then again. I could hear her breathing and the rustle of leaves. I shivered.

“You gave me a ring at the perfect time.” Her voice sounded loopy. It echoed in my ear. “Always looking out for your sister, hm?”

“Are you drunk?”

“No.” A dampened laugh.

“Is something wrong?” I asked, a tightening feeling crawling up my throat.

“No,” she said. “Nothing is wrong. I just needed to get out of this horticulture thing.”

“Must have been bad.”

She was silent on the other end. Then, a laugh. I let out a breath I didn’t know I was holding in. “Everything’s fine.”

“Alright,” I said. “Don’t push yourself.” She went quiet but I knew she was smiling through the phone. Then, the dial tone.

Perhaps I was too occupied with myself during this time to realize the strangeness of our last phone call. I was irked by her eagerness to escape a situation, and moreso, the awkwardness of what she was escaping from. Horticulture. It wasn’t a familiar word in my vocabulary and I knew it wasn’t in hers either. At least in the sister that I knew.

I desired her—the idea of her, the flesh of her, the harrowing presence of her—more than anything. Now more than ever. In those moments before her death, was she different from what I remembered? Her long black hair, those snake-like hazel eyes, and a mouth that was always curved mysteriously, like she knew your cruelest, darkest thought. She was a spitting image of our dead mother and my father loved her very much. I did too. So I held to these fragmented memories of her like it was my lifeline.

 

My roommate, Aria, was a sophomore from Chappaqua and dead set on getting a job in investment banking. She had yellow blonde curls and nothing interesting to say. When I had told her I was undecided and taking a random load of classes that didn’t even fill my general education requirements, she gave me a tight-lipped smile. We rarely spoke after that.

Our sides of the room looked very different. She had flimsy decorations and pictures with family and friends. Those cheap Christmas lights casting a warm hue along the borders of her ceiling. There was nothing that I wanted to take from home, except for my sister’s old wushu broadsword, which I positioned above the headboard of my bed as some memorial to her. If you could call it that.

I was in the bathroom when I first met Dana during Orientation Week. She was brushing her teeth, foamy mouth dripping onto the side of the sink like a dog with rabies. Kind of looked like one too with her beady eyes and upturned nose.

“You’re a freshman, huh?” She spat out the rest of her toothpaste concoction. It was bright blue and almost stained her yellowing teeth.

“Yeah.” I was rather uninterested.

“I can tell because of the way you walk.” I didn’t know what she meant by that, but it made my back straighten a bit. “Where are you from?”

“Phoenix,” I said, wiping off my eyeliner. It stung.

There was a lull. “Well, I’m from ‘round here. So if you have any questions about going into Philly or Swarthmore in general, I’m your girl.”

I stared at her in the mirror. “Thanks.”

She picked at the gap between her two front teeth and turned to me. “You do acid?”

 

The feeling wasn’t what I thought it would be. My breath slowed, that’s what I noticed first. Then, her room started to move, hued with orange and purple lights. A feeling of numbness spread throughout my body. Felt it especially on my tongue.

I started to wonder if I had made a mistake, if I was even in the right state of mind to be taking this many tabs, on my first trip too, but then the walls started to pulse and breathe alongside me and all I could do was laugh stupidly.

She called out my name.

“What?” My mouth was dry. “Did you say something?”

“Let’s go outside.”

I remembered her grabbing my arm and the feeling of her clammy palms on my forearm made my stomach churn, but I focused on the trails of lights following me and stumbled across whatever lawn she was taking me to, next to some dome-shaped building. Everything was moving.

The grass was a bit damp and it felt cool against my skin. I closed my eyes for a second and suddenly, something wet licked at my lips.

“What the fuck?”

Dana’s face was contorted in this real ugly way. Her eyes were huge. “Don’t ruin the moment.”

“Moment?” My head was spinning. I tried to push myself away from her, but everything kept coming closer. Like a dolly zoom. I could see the peach fuzz on her face in the moonlight. That scared me.

“Just enjoy it.” She grabbed my face again and mashed it against hers.

I felt my body contort with an unrestrained squirm, moving slow and fast at the same time, and I was ready to scream, wanted to scream, but I couldn’t for some reason and that alone made me stop breathing. Her wet hands pressed against my cheeks, the condensation of her sweat dirtying me and it made me want to claw at my skin until raw and I realized my eyes had been dizzyingly tightened shut the entire time, so I opened my eyes, wide and wider. Out of the corner, I saw her, not Dana, but my sister, my dead sister, but she was alive and smiling and had that Christian Lacroix tie, the one that we had gotten for our father on his 49th birthday, the one that she had taken all the credit for, draped around her neck and like always, she had that conniving grin, that one that I loved and hated, smeared across her beautiful face. I could feel her fingers on my jaw, stroking up, cold and dead. I thought she was going to kill me, kill me right then and there because sisters like us, we were always meant to be together. My throat churned and I let out a breathy shriek.

And just like that, the weight on top of me was gone. A breeze curled underneath my shirt and tickled my back. I looked up to see a pale-faced, albino girl with glowing hair staring down, but not at me. Her lips looked like they had been stained by cherries.

“There’s a lot of noise out here.”

I wiped my mouth, a trail of blood swiping across the back of my hand. Swallowed the metallic taste. Dana let out a pathetic, “Fuck, sorry.”

The girl only frowned.

Dana’s eyes were wet, dripping, and I thought mine might be too. “You don’t understand. We gotta go.” She gripped my wrist, tethering herself to me like a chain.

“I don’t want to.” The words slipped off my numb tongue before I could properly think them. “Not with you.”

“Does it look like I care?” Dana’s face cornered mine.

“You can leave,” the girl said to Dana, eyes dead. “You’re frightening her.”

Dana stumbled up and gave me one last glance. She seemed like she was going to piss herself, right then and there, and the thought of that made me laugh. I laughed until she was gone.

I looked up to the girl and gave her my best, curated smile. There was something magnetic about her and I found myself wanting to touch her translucent skin. The veins in her arms seemed to pulsate and glow in the darkness of the night. “Thank you.”

She didn’t respond. Air reached down my throat and grabbed my breath like a reckless invader. She wouldn’t look at me, and that made me attracted towards her. Something in my stomach dropped.

“What’s your name?” I stayed smiling, placatingly.

The girl glimpsed in my direction. “Marlowe.”

I looked around, suddenly hyper aware of my surroundings. The building next to us, a greenhouse, seemed alive, moving to the tempo of my own heartbeat. Only a small, red light on the other side of the circumference illuminated the space. I felt a pang in my chest. “Where am I?” I furrowed my brows, clutching onto bunches of grass to anchor myself. It felt slippery between my fingers.

“Outside of our greenhouse. The Horticulture Club’s.”

A feeling of euphoria rushed to my head. Maybe God was looking out for me after all. She looked like God herself. Did she know my sister? I wanted to ask, but that felt rash. This moment had to be anything but that.

I told her my name and asked if I could join.

“Join?” Her eyes met mine for the first time. She lifted an eyebrow. “We aren’t accepting any new members.”

Her chin was lifted slightly and I could see a birthmark hidden in the crease of her porcelain skin. I suddenly felt conscious of how I looked, the wetness of the grass seeping into my skin through my corduroy pants. A distorted feeling of humiliation inflamed in my throat.

“You’d benefit from having me in the club,” I found myself saying. “I’m incredibly loyal to the things I love the most.”

“When did I ever say anything about loyalty?”

A flurry of heat torched my insides. “You didn’t. I did.”

Marlowe looked amused. That left a tingling sensation on my cheeks. “We have enough members.” Then, she shrugged. “But if you insist.”

“I do,” I said fervently. It felt pious. “I really do.”

“Your dedication seems to be a wonderful thing,” she said, her pointer finger poking at my forehead. It was cold and I relished the feeling in the heat of the night. “Doesn’t it?”

 

I was told to go to the greenhouse the next day. I knocked on the door and when no one answered, went in. On the other side of the greenhouse, Marlowe had gloves on and was clipping a bonsai tree. She was wearing a long black dress and had her hair pulled tightly into a bun. Something inside of me burned.

There were three other people next to her, lazily watching her trim. I walked closer to them, but none of them turned their attention to me. Marlowe held the scissors delicately. A snip here, a snip there. Almost rhythmic. Every move looked calculated and planned.

The girl on the left of her had shiny black hair that went down to her tailbone with every contour of her body emphasized by her skin-tight black getup. The one on the right had dreads that stopped just below her breasts. She had earbuds in, music on the highest setting, and I could hear faintly what she was listening to. Cocteau Twins.

There was only one boy. His glasses covered most of his face, although they couldn’t hide his purpled eyebags. He was the first to notice me.

“Hi there,” he smiled. It was rather charming. “You’re our newest member, right?” He introduced himself as Max, and the two girls as Indigo and Tia, respectively.

I nodded. The other two glanced in my direction. Indigo’s eyes glittered.

“Do you know anything about horticulture?” Indigo asked, pulling out a Marlboro. She leaned into Max, who lit the cig for her. It felt like she was asserting dominance. “I’m surprised that Marlowe invited you.” She blew a ring of smoke.

Marlowe put down her scissors, darkened eyes focused on me.

I had spent the summer studying various books like Simon Schama’s Landscape and Memory that I had found in my sister’s boxes in preparation for something like this. It wasn’t that I didn’t find the material interesting, but I had to wonder how my sister had gotten involved with the club itself. Back home in Scottsdale, we were surrounded by the dry, arid desert and more than that, nothing that would ever spark an interest towards a green thumb. She had never mentioned wanting to do any sort of work with plants. It seemed odd for her to seek out a niche, rather dull activity like this. I couldn’t even imagine how she got involved with these people in the first place.

They all were staring at me now. I took an indulgent breath and gave them my memorized monologue. I had spent a great deal trying to structure it so that it felt natural, like I had been an expert in horticulture for years.

“Well,” Max said. A brief, tense pause. Only Tia’s muffled music filled the silence. “You might know more about it than me.”

“Wonderful.” Marlowe gave me a small, affirming smile. “Then let’s have some tea.”

 

The tea was not tea and I wanted to vomit. It was tea, but not the tea that I thought it would be, the kind that I wanted. It was something called Ayahuasca and it made my stomach churn. The four of them seemed fine. I was the only one suffering.

“Do you feel alright?” Indigo asked. She was sitting on Max’s lap. Her hair looked like it would consume her. “You don’t look good at all.”

“I’m fine,” I breathed out. “Totally fine.”

Tia, who still had her headphones in, put her hand on my shoulder as if to tell me to calm down. It only made me more nauseous. She had this sickly-sweet perfume on, the one that all of the expensive girls at my high school used to wear just to get fucked by old men who were their dad’s age.

I breathed out through my mouth and sniffed. Some unintelligible muttering waned in and out. The bromeliads seemed to be talking to me. I could feel their breath on the back of my neck. It was like they were on the edge of telling me something but couldn’t. That made me paranoid and more nauseous than before.

I focused on Marlowe, who had her eyes closed and was leaning back into her chair. She looked rather regal with the sunlight obstructing her face at all the right angles. I tried to center myself on her to calm down. The sharpness of her cheekbones. Her long white lashes. White eyebrows. White hair. Pale lips parted. She almost looked dead.

Her eyelids fluttered open and our eyes met. Heat rose to my cheeks, embarrassed that I had been caught, but she didn’t look away, she kept her gaze locked on me and suddenly, my entire body prickled. Self-immolation.

Marlowe shut her eyes slowly as if she were orgasming, mouth parted slightly. The warmth only burned harder. I closed my eyes, as if to mimic her.

 

They didn’t contact me for three days. I mostly spent the time in bed, glazed in a drunken stupor which I guessed was due to withdrawals from the tea. I sweated through my sheets and left a sticky dampness trail in my stead. When I was lucid enough, I would think of my sister. How I had failed where she had succeeded and how she was able to get into the club and couldn’t. Something about that made me anxious and I chewed into my nails compulsively until they exposed my pink, raw skin. I was picking the dried blood off when Tia showed up at my dorm door. Slowdive filled the empty space through her oversized headphones.

“Hi,” I said. I didn’t know why I was expecting the worst, but I held my breath anyway. She grabbed my hand and started to lead me out of the room before I jerked back instinctively. With a tilt of her head to the side, almost condescendingly, I got the sense that I wasn’t supposed to resist.

Her lips were covered in a shiny gloss, almost like they were made of glass. I couldn’t count how many layers of clothes she was wearing, some wife beater with a webbed, pistachio sweater over it, only covering one of her breasts and body jewelry that connected to her navel. I looked down at my dirty clothes and stiffened.

“Can you give me a second?” I asked. Tia nodded, but didn’t budge from the doorframe. Instead, she took a seat at my desk and stared openly. Shamelessly. I was flustered, but I tried not to show it. The only other girl I remembered changing in front of frequently had been my sister. Our little ritual went like this: I would sit in front of the vanity and she would hold the sides of my waist, and then comb through my hair with her fingers. With Tia watching me, this felt like some invasion of our little secret.

I slowly stripped myself of my sweats, awkwardly shuffling on a pair of jeans and buttoning on a blouse. My hair was knotted in the back so I brushed it straight and put it in a low ponytail that hung right below my shoulder blades. Tia’s presence made me feel strangely vulnerable, like the object of a voyeur.

Once I was finished, Tia led me silently to the greenhouse. She didn’t go in with me, waiting by the doorframe once I was noticed by Indigo, who was wrangling some leaves into a grandiose display, and then left. Indigo stopped to nudge Marlowe, who had her back facing me.

“I’m so glad you could join us,” Marlowe said, putting down an overgrown, unruly bonsai on the main table. “I would have retrieved you myself if I hadn’t had a shipment coming in.”

“Oh.” My mouth felt taut. “It’s okay.” I didn’t know what else to say.

Marlowe smiled, a bit sympathetic. A bit indulgent. She had the smallest triangular gap between her two front teeth. “I should have reached out sooner, but things just got a bit busy.” She paused. “You weren’t waiting on our call or anything, I hope.”

I stilled, though I didn’t know why. Marlowe was looking at me, staring at me with those clouded eyes of hers, and I felt as though I was frozen in time. Frozen in her trance. I could only manage a robotic shake of the head.

Indigo let out a laugh. “Marlowe’s just teasing.” The other just shrugged in response and dusted soil off one of the bonsai’s branches. “What matters is that you’re here now, right?”

I thought she was asking a rhetorical question, but then she lifted a sharp eyebrow up at me expectantly.

“Right.”

 

I religiously went to the greenhouse for the next few weeks, drinking the tea on occasion, which I acclimated to after more sessions, and learning about each of their specialties. Marlowe with her bonsai. Indigo, a master of ikebana. Tia took care of all the exotic plants in the greenhouse and managed the small zen garden east of the greenhouse. And Max fronted most of the dirty work, fertilizer, watering, and potting. I found him in the greenhouse one day with three live carps, flailing in a small tank he was peering at intensely. Maybe they knew what was going to happen to them.

I didn’t, so I asked.

“Fish guts are one of the best natural fertilizers out there,” he explained. His glasses were perched low on his nose. “I don’t know if three is enough. I should have gotten four.”

“Do you need help?”

He lifted an eyebrow. “Usually girls are squeamish about this kind of stuff. At least, Indigo is.”

His eyes were fully dilated. I lifted my sleeve. “I can help.”

He let out a hearty, pleasing laugh. “Of course you can.” He pulled out a net from the cabinet and fished out a resistant carp on to the table. Its green scales glinted and its mouth gasped for air. Asphyxiated.

 He handed me the cleaver and I found myself holding my breath. I watched the fish struggle, watched the gills pulsate with desperation. Why was it straining itself to live? More than anything, I envied it. The tail flapped on the table, like a metronome. I saw my reflection in the darkness of its eyes.

“Are you going to—”

Before he could finish, I slammed the cleaver down. The struggle stopped. Max let out a small curse. I looked down at the carnage and then, at the two living carps, waiting for their fate. There was blood all over my new silk blouse, but all I could do was exhale. A sweetness on the tip of my tongue, a feeling of exhilaration. I reached for the net.

“There was a girl last year.”

Marlowe was trimming one of her favorite trees, a Juniper. “Who?” A snip.

“I heard she was in this club.” Another snip. “And that she committed suicide.”

“Okay.” Two snips. Then, Marlowe put her clippers on the table. She took off her gloves and let her hair down.

“Do you know why?”

Marlowe gave me a look. “Why would I?”

“I don’t know. Because she was in this club. And you’re all rather close.”

She pondered for a second. “Why does anybody kill themselves?”

“I guess people are unhappy with their lives.” 

“So let them be unhappy.”

Marlowe’s avoidance of my question made me feel uneasy. I also wasn’t sure if my sister had been unhappy, not in the conventional sense at least. Everything she wanted, she got. To think she wanted to die—I couldn’t imagine it. I couldn’t justify it.

She put back on her gloves to signify that the conversation was over. She resumed her snipping. I looked over at Tia desperately. She was listening to Beethoven’s Moonlight Sonata, 3rd movement, and stared at me blankly. Like I had broken something I shouldn't have touched in the first place.

 

I found Max and Indigo in the greenhouse the next day. The two were rather elusive, but almost always were together. Always touching like two lovesick teenagers.

That day, Max was in charge of tidying up the greenhouse. He was carrying out all the clippings to the dumpster a few steps away, trudging through the muddied orange leaves that were starting to fall from the trees. I sat beside Indigo, in her signature tight black suit. She was watching Max with hawk eyes. I had asked Marlowe if they were dating, because it seemed rather obvious that they were, but she laughed at the idea, as if I were making the most ridiculous observation in the world.

“I didn’t really like you at first,” Indigo told me as we watched Max drag one of the last garbage bags out of the greenhouse.

“I couldn’t tell,” I said.

She swatted my arm. “I don’t like most people. It was supposed to be a compliment.”

“Thanks, I guess.”

“I’m serious,” she said. “You’re much better than that bitch we had before.”

Pursed lips and glazed eyes, tense with disdain, as if she were recalling a memory that she wished she hadn’t. “Who?”

“Oh.” Indigo kept her eyes on Max through the glass. “You know.”

“I don’t.”

“We didn’t like her too much. She couldn’t handle it.” There was a husk to her voice, a biting coldness. She stiffened. “Anyways, it doesn’t matter.”

“Handle what?”

My curiosity slipped through my tongue. I felt so close to the truth, I just needed a bit more. But then the air shifted and Indigo was looking at me, frowning. I held my breath as she scanned my entire face slowly, from the top of my forehead all the way down to the tip of my chin, as if she were memorizing every feature. Then, she looked back at Max, who gazed over at us through the obscured glass, and let out a delighted, satisfied laugh.

“Don’t worry about it.”

 

Marlowe and I had dinner at her place later that night. The steak we were eating was blue rare. Marlowe and the others had primarily meat-based diets, which was a bit odd considering their affinity towards plants. I cut into the steak, the bloodied juice pouring out. I had never liked the taste.

“Why did you ask me that yesterday?”

I paused, mid-bite. There was a mirror behind her and I could see part of my reflection, steak glistening in the light on my fork. “What?”

Marlowe looked at me delicately. “It was odd. Out of character for you.”

“What is in character for me?” I laughed. She didn’t. I stopped.

“Keeping your head down.”

She was staring at me, but her eyes were dead. It reminded me of the first time we had met, the first time she had seen right through me. Like I was invisible. That feeling made me recoil back into myself. I sipped the wine nervously, my fingers unable to keep a steady grip on the glass.

“I don’t think I know the real you.”

I tried to keep a straight face. “What do you mean?”

Marlowe didn’t answer, just half-heartedly shrugged. It was silent for a beat, and then two. The tone of our conversation seemed to take a hard turn into something else, and I didn’t want to find out what it was.

“Let’s talk about something else,” I tried. “I don’t feel well.”

“What is there to talk about?”

“Everything,” I said. “Anything.”

“I want to talk about you. The real you.”

“This is the real me,” I said, a little desperately. “Not some game—”

“Isn’t it?”

I opened and closed my mouth dumbly. “What?”

“A game to you,” she said, tracing the outline of her plate. “I thought you really cared for us, you know.”

All of the feeling in my face went numb, as if I had been dunked into an ice bath.

“I do,” I swallowed. The words felt heavy in my mouth, as if I had to manually lift up my tongue for every syllable. “I really care about you.”

“No.” She closed her eyes, eyelashes pointed down. “You’re disloyal. Or maybe it was that you were never loyal in the first place.”

I let out a shaky breath. Nothing felt worse than this. “Marlowe, please.”

“We never did anything to her.” Her voice was strange. Calm. “She killed herself all on her own.”

“I never said you did, I just said—”

“I know what you said.”

The silence was deafening and a bassy sound flooded my eardrums, turning them hot and raw. It felt as if I was underwater, drowning in my own thick blood. I didn’t know what to say with her looking at me like that, knowing me like that and more importantly, didn’t know what she wanted me to say. My vision blurred and I felt as though I would pass out, a sickening feeling crawling up my throat and sliding down to my toes.

“I just wanted to know,” I said quietly.

Marlowe got up from her chair and put her hands on my shoulders. I couldn’t see her face through the mirror, only myself, but watched as her hands smoothed over my hair. I couldn’t help but feel comforted in some blurred way. I wonder if she knew.

“She died because she wanted to.” Marlowe’s breath was cool on my skin. I hadn’t realized that I had been burning up the entire time. “We just helped her realize it.”

Slowly, Marlowe thumbed over my lips. I could taste blood on her skin and the thought of where it had come from made me shiver. I stared at myself through the mirror, but I couldn’t recognize the person staring back. And in some distorted way, I knew it was her. My sister. She was giving me that look, that coded smile of hers and I saw it in her twinkling, dark stare. I wondered what she would have wanted me to do. I wondered a lot of things, but then, I thought it was better not to think at all. I closed my eyes.

Marlowe’s hands slowly crawled from the back of my ears to my neck, icy fingers lightly lacing around my throat and exerting a pressure that was so subtle, I almost didn’t feel it. I leaned into the tightening feeling. The vulnerability, the control. I didn’t know if it was right to live this way, but I had never been concerned with what was right before.

She took my face into her cold hands and I presented my head devoutly.
